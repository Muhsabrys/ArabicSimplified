Interactive Guide to Arabic Prepositions

حُرُوفُ الْجَرِّ (ḥurūf al-jarr)

This guide provides an interactive way to explore the most common prepositions in Arabic. Select a preposition from the list to learn about its meaning, usage, and see it in action with examples.

في

fī "in," "at," "on"

Indicates location (inside, at, on), time (during), or a general context.

أَنَا فِي الْبَيْتِ

Anā fī al-bayti

"I am in the house."

فِي الصَّبَاحِ

Fī aṣ-ṣabāḥi

"In the morning."

فِي هَذَا الْكِتَابِ

Fī hādhā al-kitābi

"In this book."

إِلَى

ilā "to," "towards," "until"

Indicates direction, a destination, or a limit in time or space.

أَذْهَبُ إِلَى الْمَدْرَسَةِ

Adhhabu ilā al-madrasati

"I go to the school."

مِنَ الصَّبَاحِ إِلَى الْمَسَاءِ

Mina aṣ-ṣabāḥi ilā al-masāʾi

"From morning until evening."

مِنْ

min "from," "of," "some"

Indicates origin, source, being part of a whole, or cause.

جِئْتُ مِنَ السُّوقِ

Jiʾtu mina as-sūqi

"I came from the market."

كَأْسٌ مِنَ الْمَاءِ

Kaʾsun mina al-māʾi

"A glass of water."

بَعْضٌ مِنَ الطُّلَّابِ

Baʿḍun mina aṭ-ṭullābi

"Some of the students."

عَلَى

ʿalā "on," "upon," "over," "against"

Indicates position (on top of), obligation, or sometimes opposition.

الْكِتَابُ عَلَى الطَّاوِلَةِ

Al-kitābu ʿalā aṭ-ṭāwilati

"The book is on the table."

وَاجِبٌ عَلَيْهِ

Wājibun ʿalayhi

"It is an obligation upon him."

لِـ

li- / la- "for," "to," "belonging to"

Indicates possession, purpose, or an indirect object. It attaches directly to the following word.

هَذَا لِي

Hādhā lī

"This is for me / This belongs to me."

قُلْتُ لَهُ

Qultu lahu

"I said to him."

بِـ

bi- "with," "by means of," "in"

Indicates means, accompaniment, reason, or location/time. It attaches directly to the following word.

كَتَبْتُ بِالْقَلَمِ

Katabtu bil-qalami

"I wrote with the pen."

بِاسْمِ اللهِ

Bi-ismi Allāhi

"In the name of God."

سَافَرْتُ بِالْقِطَارِ

Sāfartu bil-qiṭāri

"I traveled by train."

كَـ

ka- "like," "as"

Indicates resemblance or comparison. It attaches directly to the following word.

أَنْتَ كَالْأَسَدِ

Anta kal-asadi

"You are like a lion."

كَمَا قُلْتُ

Kamā qultu

"As I said."

عَنْ

ʿan "about," "from," "away from"

Indicates separation, the topic of discussion, or distance.

تَكَلَّمْتُ عَنِ الْقِصَّةِ

Takallamtu ʿani al-qiṣṣati

"I spoke about the story."

اِبْتَعَدَ عَنِ الْخَطَرِ

Ibtataʿada ʿani al-khaṭari

"He moved away from the danger."

Key Characteristics

A core rule of Arabic grammar is that any noun or pronoun that follows a preposition must be in the genitive case (majrūr - مَجْرُور). This is often indicated by a kasra (i) vowel ending on definite nouns.
Prepositions can attach directly to pronoun suffixes to form a single word. For example, إِلَى (ilā) + هُ (hu) becomes إِلَيْهِ (ilayhi), meaning "to him". Similarly, فِي (fī) + هَا (hā) becomes فِيهَا (fīhā), meaning "in her".
Similar to phrasal verbs in English, many Arabic verbs are paired with specific prepositions to achieve their intended meaning. For instance, the verb بَحَثَ (baḥatha) means "to discuss," but when paired with عَنْ (ʿan), the phrase بَحَثَ عَنْ (baḥatha ʿan) means "to search for." Learning these combinations is key to fluency.
