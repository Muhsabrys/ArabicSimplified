Introduction

Scripts, not fonts

In the Latin-alphabet world, we are used to thinking in terms of "fonts" — interchangeable digital dress that can be swapped over the same letterforms without changing what the writing fundamentally is. Arabic calligraphy does not work this way. Arabic tradition speaks instead of khaṭṭ (خَطّ), meaning "line" or "script": a complete system of proportion, letter construction, and stroke order, codified over centuries by master calligraphers and transmitted through apprenticeship, not simply chosen from a menu.

Each script — Kufic, Naskh, Thuluth, and the rest — has its own geometry. The classical scripts are built on a module derived from the rhombic dot made by a reed pen (qalam) cut at a particular angle: the height of the letter alif, the curve of a nun, the proportions of every other letter are measured against that dot. Changing script means changing the underlying rules of construction, not just the outward appearance — closer to the difference between italic handwriting and engineering blueprint lettering than to switching between two typefaces of the same alphabet.

Over time, distinct scripts developed for distinct purposes: monumental scripts for stone and stucco, book scripts for copying the Qurʾan and scholarly texts, chancery scripts for royal decrees, and swift cursive hands for everyday correspondence. What follows is an overview of ten of the most historically significant styles.

"The pen is the tongue of the hand, and its writing is the sweetest speech." — a maxim often repeated in classical calligraphic treatises, underscoring that in Islamic civilisation, writing itself was considered an art on par with poetry.

The scripts

Ten major calligraphic styles

Each card gives the script's names, background, defining features, common uses, and a sample phrase set in the closest available digital typeface.

الخط الكوفي Kufic
7th–10th c.+

The oldest formal Arabic script, traditionally associated with the garrison city of Kufa in Iraq, though its angular forms likely evolved from earlier Nabataean and Syriac-influenced writing. Kufic dominated Qurʾanic transcription for roughly its first three centuries and later persisted as a decorative, monumental script long after cursive hands took over book-copying.

Visual characteristics

  • Angular, geometric strokes built on straight lines and sharp turns
  • Heavy horizontal emphasis, short vertical strokes
  • Many regional and decorative variants: floriated, plaited, square (bannaʾi)

Common uses

  • Early Qurʾanic manuscripts on parchment
  • Monumental architectural inscriptions and coinage
  • Later revived purely as ornament on textiles, ceramics, and buildings

بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ

"In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful" — the phrase most often rendered monumentally in Kufic.

Rendered in Reem Kufi, a modern geometric revival of Kufic forms.

خط النسخ Naskh
10th c. onward

Naskh (literally "copying") was refined in the 10th century, most notably by the vizier and calligrapher Ibn Muqla, who formalised proportional rules based on the rhombic dot and the circle. Its clarity made it the preferred hand for copying manuscripts, and it remains the direct ancestor of most printed and digital Arabic type today.

Visual characteristics

  • Rounded, evenly proportioned letters with short verticals
  • High legibility even at small sizes
  • Even baseline with modest, controlled curves

Common uses

  • The dominant script for Qurʾans since the 11th century
  • Books, newspapers, and nearly all printed and digital Arabic text
  • General everyday reading material

العِلْمُ نُورٌ

"Knowledge is light" — a proverb commonly set in Naskh for its clarity.

Rendered in Noto Naskh Arabic.

خط الثلث Thuluth
11th c. onward

Thuluth ("a third") takes its name from the proportion of straight to curved strokes in its original formulation. Developed in the Abbasid period and elevated to an art of its own by later Ottoman masters, it became the script of grandeur — large, majestic, and demanding of considerable skill, often reserved for the most prestigious commissions.

Visual characteristics

  • Tall vertical strokes with long, sweeping curves
  • Letters overlap and interlace, often stacked vertically
  • Highly ornamental, slower and more difficult to execute than Naskh

Common uses

  • Mosque domes, minarets, and monumental architectural bands
  • Qurʾan chapter headings and illuminated titles
  • Ottoman calligraphic panels (levha) and official seals (tughra) elements

لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا اللَّه

"There is no god but God" — a declaration frequently monumentalised in Thuluth on mosque walls.

Rendered in Amiri Quran, bolded to suggest Thuluth's sweeping weight (closest available approximation).

الخط الديواني Diwani
15th–16th c.

Developed in the Ottoman chancery (diwan) from the 15th century and closely guarded as a court secret for a time, Diwani was designed partly for its dense, intricate letterforms, which made official documents difficult to forge or alter. A more elaborate variant, Diwani Jali, packs the background between letters with decorative dots and flourishes.

Visual characteristics

  • Steeply sloped, tightly interwoven letters with minimal straight strokes
  • Ascending baseline, dense clustering of words
  • Diwani Jali variant fills negative space with ornamental marks

Common uses

  • Ottoman royal decrees (firmans) and state correspondence
  • Ceremonial certificates and formal decorative panels
  • Modern logo and title design seeking an ornate, regal feel

دَامَتْ عِزَّتُهُ

"May his glory endure" — a courtly phrase typical of Ottoman chancery formulas.

Rendered in Aref Ruqaa, italicised (closest available approximation — no digital font fully captures Diwani's interlace).

خط الرقعة Ruqʿah
19th c.

Standardised in the Ottoman civil service during the 19th century, Ruqʿah was designed for speed and simplicity — a practical hand for clerks and ordinary writers rather than an art form for display. It is today the script most people across the Arab world actually learn to handwrite.

Visual characteristics

  • Short, simple strokes with minimal curvature
  • Compact letterforms, straight and economical connections
  • Few decorative flourishes; built for legibility at speed

Common uses

  • Everyday personal handwriting across the Arab world
  • School instruction as the standard cursive hand
  • Informal notes, letters, and quick correspondence

مع أطيب التحيات

"With best regards" — a everyday closing phrase, exactly the register Ruqʿah was made for.

Rendered in Aref Ruqaa, a digital typeface named for and modelled on this script.

خط النستعليق Nastaliq
14th–15th c.

Developed in Iran, Nastaliq's name blends Naskh and the earlier Taʿliq script. Though it uses the Arabic alphabet, it became the definitive script of Persian and, later, Urdu literature, prized for its lyrical, flowing quality that mirrors the poetry it was so often used to record.

Visual characteristics

  • Short verticals, elongated horizontal strokes that hang below the baseline
  • Words appear to cascade diagonally across the line
  • Extremely fluid, favouring curvature over straight strokes

Common uses

  • Classical Persian poetry manuscripts (Rumi, Hafez, Ferdowsi)
  • Urdu literature, newspapers, and print until recent decades
  • Miniature painting captions and album inscriptions

دل به دلبر داد

"[The] heart gave itself to the beloved" — a lyrical fragment in the register Nastaliq was built to carry.

Rendered in Noto Nastaliq Urdu, a digital typeface purpose-built for this script.

الخط المغربي Maghrebi
10th c. onward

Evolved in the western Islamic lands — Al-Andalus, the Maghreb, and later West Africa — from early Kufic roots along a separate path from the eastern scripts codified by Ibn Muqla. It preserved distinctive letterforms even as Naskh and Thuluth came to dominate the east.

Visual characteristics

  • Rounded bowls and generous loops that extend well below the baseline
  • A distinctive final nun and qaf dotted differently from eastern scripts
  • Full, flowing curves with a relaxed, open rhythm

Common uses

  • Andalusian and North African Qurʾan manuscripts
  • West African (Sudani/Suqi variants) religious and scholarly texts
  • Regional administrative and literary documents historically

بَارَكَ اللَّهُ فِيكُم

"May God bless you" — a common devotional phrase found in Maghrebi manuscript colophons.

Rendered in Lateef, chosen for its open, rounded forms (closest available approximation).

خط المحقق Muhaqqaq
13th–15th c.

One of the six classical "proportioned" scripts (al-aqlam al-sittah) codified after Ibn Muqla's system, Muhaqqaq reached its height under the Mamluks, prized for producing some of the largest and most magnificent Qurʾan manuscripts ever made, their pages sometimes exceeding a metre in height.

Visual characteristics

  • Large scale, generous horizontal spacing between letters
  • Bold, stately, deliberately unhurried strokes
  • Sharp, angular terminal flourishes on certain letters

Common uses

  • Grand Mamluk-era Qurʾan manuscripts commissioned for royal endowments
  • Large-format religious texts intended for ceremonial display
  • Largely fell out of everyday use after the 15th century

وَقُل رَبِّ زِدْنِي عِلْمًا

"And say: My Lord, increase me in knowledge" (Qurʾan 20:114) — the register of grand Mamluk manuscripts.

Rendered in Amiri Quran (closest available approximation of this classical proportioned script).

خط الريحاني Rayhani
13th–15th c.

Often described as a smaller, more delicate sibling of Muhaqqaq, Rayhani ("sweet basil") shares its structural proportions but at a reduced, finer scale, giving it a lighter, more refined presence on the page — another of the six classical proportioned scripts.

Visual characteristics

  • Same proportional rules as Muhaqqaq, applied at smaller size
  • Finer strokes, tighter letter spacing than its larger relative
  • Elegant, disciplined, precise curves

Common uses

  • Qurʾan manuscripts, particularly marginal and interlinear text
  • Fine book copying where Muhaqqaq would be too large
  • Often paired with Muhaqqaq in the same manuscript for hierarchy

سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ

"Glory be to Him Who took [His servant] by night" (Qurʾan 17:1) — the opening of Surat al-Isra.

Rendered in Amiri, medium weight, to suggest Rayhani's finer scale relative to Muhaqqaq (closest available approximation).

خط التوقيع Tawqiʿ
10th c. onward

Tawqiʿ ("signature" or "endorsement") emerged in the Abbasid chancery as a compact cursive hand for administrative use, historically considered a smaller relative of Thuluth. It was used by officials to sign and endorse state documents, later feeding into the development of Ruqʿah.

Visual characteristics

  • Compact, cursive, with dense ligatures between letters
  • Less formal and slower than Thuluth, but more ornamented than Ruqʿah
  • Efficient for writing at length while retaining some elegance

Common uses

  • Abbasid and later chancery correspondence and endorsements
  • Administrative and legal documentation
  • A historical bridge toward the modern Ruqʿah hand

صَحَّ وَتَمَّ

"Verified and completed" — a chancery endorsement formula historically written in Tawqiʿ.

In closing

Why calligraphy matters

Arabic calligraphy developed such elaborate variety in part because, within classical Islamic visual culture, figural representation played a limited role in religious contexts, and the written word — above all the text of the Qurʾan — carried enormous devotional and artistic weight. Copying scripture beautifully was itself understood as an act of piety, and calligraphers held a status closer to master craftsmen or scholars than to mere scribes.

Beyond religion, these scripts recorded the full range of civic and intellectual life: philosophy, medicine, poetry, correspondence, and law were all shaped by the script chosen to carry them, since script itself signalled register, formality, and even trustworthiness — a Diwani-scrawled decree was, quite literally, harder to forge than one in ordinary hand.

A single tradition thus produced scripts for the mosque dome, the royal court, the scholar's desk, and the everyday letter — each fit precisely to its purpose.

Today, calligraphy remains a living art. Contemporary calligraphers continue to train for years in the classical proportional systems, and the scripts described here inform everything from mosque architecture to graphic design, branding, and digital typography across the Arabic-writing world — a continuous thread running from the 7th century to the present.
