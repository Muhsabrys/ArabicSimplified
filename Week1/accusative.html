Arabic Grammar Explained

Understanding Why Word Endings Change

🎯 The Core Idea

In Arabic, the end of a word changes to show its job in a sentence. This is called grammatical case. It's a powerful system that adds precision, using special endings instead of just word order.

💡 Concept 1: Tanwin (تنوين)

Tanwin is a mark on the last letter that adds an 'n' sound. Its main job is to show that a noun is indefinite (like "a" or "an" in English).

كتابٌ (kitābun) - Nominative

كتابٍ (kitābin) - Genitive

كتابًا (kitāban) - Accusative

💡 Concept 2: Definite vs. Indefinite

This distinction is key to understanding Tanwin. A noun is either one or the other.

Definite (المعرفة)

Specific ("the..."). Starts with الـ and has NO Tanwin. e.g., الكتابُ

Indefinite (النكرة)

Non-specific ("a..."). Does NOT start with الـ and uses Tanwin. e.g., كتابٌ

The Accusative Case: الاسم المنصوب

This is one of the three main grammatical cases. A noun becomes "Mansoub" (accusative) for several key reasons, and its ending changes to a fatHa or Tanwin FatHa to show this.

Why Does a Noun Become Mansoub?

1. Direct Object (المفعول به)

The noun that receives the action of the verb.

قَرَأْتُ كِتَابًا.

"I read a book." (Indefinite)

شَرِبَ حَلِيبًا.

"He drank milk." (Indefinite)

شاهدتُ مباراةً جَيِّدة.

"I watched a good match." (Indefinite)

فَتَحَتْ البابَ.

"She opened the door." (Definite)

أَكَلَ التُّفَّاحَةَ.

"He ate the apple." (Definite)

أخذنا وقتًا طويلًا.

"We took a long time." (Indefinite)

2. Adverb of Time/Place (ظرف)

The noun that tells you *when* or *where* an action occurs.

سَنَقْضِي يَوْمًا وَاحِدًا.

"We will spend one day."

اِنْتَظَرْتُ سَاعَةً.

"I waited for an hour."

سَارَ شَمَالًا.

"He walked northwards."

زُرْتُهُ لَيْلًا.

"I visited him at night."

3. State or Condition (الحال)

The noun that describes the state of the subject/object during the action.

عَادَ الطَّالِبُ فَرِحًا.

"The student returned happy."

دَخَلَتِ الْغُرْفَةَ مُبْتَسِمَةً.

"She entered the room smiling."

جَاءُوا ضَاحِكِينَ.

"They came laughing."

ذهبتُ إلى السينما مُسرِعًا.

"I went to the cinema quickly."

أكلنا الطعامَ جميعًا.

"We ate the food all together."

4. Quantifier (التمييز)

A noun that clarifies the quantity or measure of something ambiguous before it.

كم بلدًا زُرتِ يا سارة؟

"How many countries did you visit, Sarah?"

زرتُ عشرين بلدًا.

"I visited twenty countries."

وكم شهرًا قضيتي هناك؟

"And how many months did you spend there?"

قضيتُ شهرًا واحدًا.

"I spent one month."

5. Predicate of Kana (خبر كان)

The descriptive part of a sentence starting with كان (was/were) or its sisters.

كان الطقسُ بارِدًا.

"The weather was cold."

كانت السماء مُمطِرَةً.

"The sky was rainy."

كان الوقتُ مُتأخِرًا.

"The time was late."
