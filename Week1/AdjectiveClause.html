What is جملة الصفة?

In Arabic, جملة الصفة (Jumlat As-Sifah) translates to "the adjective sentence." It acts exactly like a relative clause in English (sentences using who, which, or that).

However, Arabic achieves this simply by placing a complete sentence directly after an indefinite noun, without using words like "who" or "which".

English Structure

I have a friend who studies Middle East history.

English requires the relative pronoun "who" to link the clause to the noun.

Arabic Structure

لي صديق يدرس تاريخ الشرق الأوسط.

I have a friend [he] studies Middle East history.

Notice the absence of "who". The sentence "he studies..." directly follows the indefinite noun "friend".

The Golden Rule

A sentence functions as جملة الصفة ONLY when the noun it describes is نكرة (indefinite).

If the noun is definite (starts with الـ or is a specific name), Arabic must use a relative pronoun like الذي (who/which) to connect the sentence.

1. The Hidden Subject Pronoun

Because the defining sentence is a complete thought, it must contain a pronoun (الضمير الرابط) that links back to the indefinite noun.

When the noun acts as the subject of the new clause, the linking pronoun is usually hidden inside the conjugated verb.

...a professor [who] (he) teaches ...
... أستاذ يدرّس ...

Hover over the English verb section to see the hidden pronoun link in Arabic. The "he" is inside the verb يدرّس (yudarris).

...a woman [who] (she) works ...
... امرأة تعمل ...
4

2. Attached Object Pronouns

If the described noun is the object of the verb within the descriptive clause, the linking pronoun cannot be hidden. It must be explicitly attached as an object suffix (e.g., ـه, ـها, ـهم).

شاهدت مباراةً استمتعت بــها.

I saw a game I enjoyed [it].

Breakdown: "Game" (مباراة) is feminine. The clause "I enjoyed" (استمتعت بـ) needs an object to be complete. We attach "it" (ها) to link back to the game.
هذه أول سيجارة دخّنتــها.

This is the first cigarette I smoked [it].

5

3. Linking via Prepositions

Similarly, if the described noun acts as the object of a preposition within the clause, the linking pronoun attaches directly to that preposition. The textbook calls this "filling the hole left by the topic noun."

هذا أكبر سوقٍ ذهبت إليــه!

This is the biggest market I've ever been to [it]!

Important Difference:

In English, we can end a sentence with a preposition ("...been to.").
In Arabic, a preposition cannot be left hanging. It must have an object. Since "market" was moved to the front, we replace it with the pronoun ـه (him/it) attached to إلى (to) -> إليه.

6

4. Nominal Clauses (الجملة الاسمية)

The describing sentence does not have to be a verb. It can be a Nominal Sentence (الجملة الاسمية)—a sentence starting with a noun.
This structure is often used to translate the English word "whose". The linking pronoun usually attaches to the first noun of the embedded clause.

...a picture whose age is over 100 years.
... صورة عمرها أكبر من ١٠٠ سنة.
(Literally: A picture, her age is greater than 100 years)
I have a friend whose name is Laila.
لي صديقة اسمها ليلى.
(Literally: To me a friend, her name is Laila)
