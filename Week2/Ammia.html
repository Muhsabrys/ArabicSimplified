MSA vs. Egyptian Arabic

Understanding the Key Linguistic Differences for Beginners

Linguistic Aspects

Welcome, Beginner Arabic Learner!

It's great you're diving into Arabic! Understanding the differences between Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) and Egyptian Arabic is a fantastic step, as it will help you navigate both formal contexts and everyday conversations in Egypt.

Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) is the formal, written Arabic used in media, literature, and formal speeches across the Arab world. Egyptian Arabic (Masri) is the most widely spoken and understood colloquial dialect, primarily used for daily communication in Egypt. While they share a common root, they diverge significantly in pronunciation, grammar, and vocabulary.

Use the tabs on the left to explore the key linguistic differences. Each section includes a detailed explanation, a comparison table, and a short quiz to test your understanding!

1. Pronunciation Differences

Egyptian Arabic exhibits several notable phonetic shifts compared to MSA, especially concerning specific letters.

Letter Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) Egyptian Arabic
ث (thāʾ)

Voiceless interdental fricative, like "th" in "thin" or "three."

  • Example: ثلج (thalj - snow)
  • Example: ثلاث (thalāth - three)
  • Example: مثلث (muthallath - triangle)

Generally pronounced as س (sīn).

  • Example: تلج (talg - snow)
  • Example: تلاتة (talāta - three)
  • Example: مثلث (musallas - triangle)
ظ (ẓāʾ)

Voiced interdental fricative, like "th" in "that" or "they" (emphatic counterpart of ذ).

  • Example: ظريف (ẓarīf - charming/nice)
  • Example: عظيم (ʿaẓīm - great/magnificent)
  • Example: ظهر (ẓahr - back/noon)

Generally pronounced as ز (zāy). Often 'z' but can be 'ẓ' in educated speech. For 'ẓahr', it's commonly ضهر (ḍahr) with a heavy 'd' sound.

  • Example: ظريف (zarīf - charming/nice)
  • Example: عظيم (ʿazīm - great/magnificent)
  • Example: ضهر (ḍahr - back/noon)
ذ (dhāl)

Voiced interdental fricative, like "th" in "this" or "then."

  • Example: ذهب (dhahab - gold)
  • Example: لذيذ (ladheedh – delicious)
  • Example: أستاذ (ustādh - professor/teacher)

Generally pronounced as د (dāl) or ز (zāy).

  • Example: دهب (dahab - gold)
  • Example: لذيذ (lazeez – delicious)
  • Example: أستاذ (ustāz - professor/teacher)
ق (qāf)

Voiceless uvular stop, a guttural 'k' sound made further back in the throat.

  • Example: قلب (qalb - heart)
  • Example: قوي (qawī - strong)
  • Example: قام (qām - he stood up)

Generally pronounced as a glottal stop (ʾalif hamza ء), similar to the sound between the syllables in "uh-oh."

  • Example: قلب (ʾalb - heart)
  • Example: قوي (ʾawī - strong)
  • Example: قام (ʾām - he stood up)
ج (jīm)

Voiced palato-alveolar affricate, like "j" in "jump" or "gin."

  • Example: جمل (jamal - camel)
  • Example: جديد (jadīd - new)
  • Example: جامد (jāmed - solid)

Generally pronounced as a hard g (like in "go" or "game").

  • Example: جمل (gamal - camel)
  • Example: جديد (gidīd - new)
  • Example: جامِد (gamed - solid)
Tanween

The "n" sound added to the end of indefinite nouns (e.g., -an, -in, -un).

  • Example: كتابًا (kitāban - a book, accusative indefinite)

Almost entirely absent.

  • Example: كتاب (kitāb - a book)
Hamza (ء)

Used in various positions.

  • Example: سماء (samāʾ - sky)
  • Example: بدأ (badaʾa - he began)

Often simplified or dropped, especially in the middle or at the end of words.

  • Example: سما (sama - sky)
  • Example: بدأ (badaʾ or badā - he began)

2. Demonstratives (This, That)

Demonstratives vary significantly, especially in number and gender, between MSA and Egyptian Arabic. Egyptian Arabic has a much simpler system.

Category Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) Egyptian Arabic
Near (This/These)
  • هذا (hādhā) - This (masculine singular)
  • هذه (hādhīhi) - This (feminine singular)
  • هذان (hādhān) - These (masculine dual)
  • هاتان (hātān) - These (feminine dual)
  • هؤلاء (hāʾulāʾi) - These (human plural)
  • هذه (hādhīhi) - These (non-human plural)
  • Example: هذا كتابٌ. (Hādhā kitābun - This is a book.)
  • Example: هؤلاء طلابٌ. (Hāʾulāʾi ṭullābun - These are students.)
  • ده (da) - This (masculine singular)
  • دي (di) - This (feminine singular)
  • دول (dōl) - These (plural - all genders, human & non-human)
  • Example: ده كتاب. (Da kitāb - This is a book.)
  • Example: دول طلبة. (Dōl ṭalaba - These are students.)
Far (That/Those)
  • ذلك (dhālika) - That (masculine singular)
  • تلك (tilka) - That (feminine singular)
  • ذانك (dhānika) - Those (masculine dual)
  • تانك (tānika) - Those (feminine dual)
  • أولئك (ʾulāʾika) - Those (human plural)
  • تلك (tilka) - Those (non-human plural)
  • Example: ذلك الرجل. (Dhālika ar-rajulu - That man.)
  • Example: أولئك النساء. (ʾUlāʾika an-nisāʾu - Those women.)
  • Egyptian Arabic typically uses the same simplified forms for 'that'/'those' as for 'this'/'these', relying on context.
  • Often followed by the object itself without explicit 'that' if the object is far.
  • Example: ده كتاب. (Da kitāb - That is a book. - same as 'this')
  • Example: دول طلبة. (Dōl ṭalaba - Those are students. - same as 'these')

3. Personal and Object Pronouns

The forms of personal and object pronouns are generally similar, but Egyptian Arabic features some phonetic changes and simplifications, particularly in plural forms.

  • أنتِ (anti) - You (feminine singular)
  • هو (huwa) - He
  • هي (hiya) - She
  • نحن (naḥnu) - We
  • أنتم (antum) - You (masculine plural)
  • أنتن (antunna) - You (feminine plural)
  • هم (hum) - They (masculine plural)
  • هن (hunna) - They (feminine plural)
  • Example: أنا أذهب. (Anā adhhab - I go.)

  • إنتَ (inta) - You (masculine singular)
  • إنتِ (inti) - You (feminine singular)
  • هو (howa) - He

  • إنتوا (intu) - You (plural - both genders)
  • هُمَّ (homma) - They (plural - both genders)

4. Relative Pronouns (Who, That, Which)

This is a significant simplification in Egyptian Arabic, which uses a single, invariant form.

Category Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) Egyptian Arabic
Forms

Agree in gender and number with their antecedent.

  • الذي (alladhī) - who, which, that (masculine singular)
  • التي (allatī) - who, which, that (feminine singular)
  • اللذان (alladhān) - who, which, that (masculine dual)
  • اللتان (allatān) - who, which, that (feminine dual)
  • الذين (alladhīna) - who, which, that (masculine plural)
  • اللاتي / اللواتي (allātī / allawātī) - who, which, that (feminine plural)
  • Example: هذا هو الرجل الذي رأيته. (Hādhā huwa ar-rajulu alladhī raʾaytuhu - This is the man whom I saw.)
  • Example: هذه هي السيارة التي اشتريتها. (Hādhīhi hiya as-sayyāratu allatī ishtaraytuhā - This is the car which I bought.)

A single, invariant form for all genders, numbers, and cases.

  • اللي (illi) - who, which, that (all forms)
  • Example: ده الراجل اللي شفته. (Da er-rāgil illi shoftoh - This is the man whom I saw.)
  • Example: دي العربية اللي اشتريتها. (Di el-ʿarabeya illi eshtarētaha - This is the car which I bought.)

5. Verb Conjugation in the Present Tense

Egyptian Arabic often drops the initial "a" for the first person singular and uses a specific prefix (`ب-`) unique to the dialect to indicate continuous or habitual action. MSA uses specific prefixes and suffixes, and distinct moods.

Aspect Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) Egyptian Arabic
General Structure

Uses specific prefixes and suffixes for persons, and distinct moods (indicative, subjunctive, jussive).

  • Prefixes: أ- (a- for I), ت- (ta- for you/she), ي- (ya- for he/they), ن- (na- for we)

Uses the specific prefix ب- (ba-/bi-) for all persons to indicate continuous or habitual action. Moods are largely absent.
Example: To Go

MSA (يذهب):

  • أذهب (adhhab - I go)
  • تذهب (tadhhab - you (masc. sg.) go / she goes)
  • يذهب (yadhhab - he goes)
  • تذهبون (tadhhabūna - you (masc. pl.) go)
  • يذهبون (yadhhabūna - they (masc. pl.) go)

Egyptian (يروح):

  • أنا باروح (ana barūḥ - I go/I am going)
  • إنتَ بتروح (inta betrūḥ - you (masc. sg.) go/are going)
  • هو بيروح (howa beyrūḥ - he goes/is going)
  • إحنا بنروح (eḥna benrūḥ - we go/are going)
  • هم بيروحوا (hom beyrūḥu - they go/are going)
Example Sentence
  • أنا أكتبُ رسالةً. (Anā aktubu risālatan - I am writing a letter.)
  • هي تدرسُ العربية. (Hiya tadrusu al-ʿArabiyyah - She is studying Arabic.)
  • أنا باكتب جواب. (Ana bakteb gawāb - I am writing a letter.)
  • هي بتدرس عربي. (Heya betderes ʿarabi - She is studying Arabic.)

7. Past Tense Formation

The core conjugations for the past tense are generally similar between MSA and Egyptian Arabic, but pronunciation differences significantly affect the sound of the conjugated verbs.

Aspect Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) Egyptian Arabic
Formation

Formed by adding specific suffixes to the verb root.

Generally very similar to MSA in terms of suffixes, but with Egyptian phonetic shifts (e.g., Qaf to glottal stop).
Example: To Go

MSA (ذهب):

  • ذهبتُ (dhahabtu - I went)
  • ذهبتَ (dhahabta - you (masc. sg.) went)
  • ذهبَ (dhahaba - he went)
  • ذهبوا (dhahabū - they (masc. pl.) went)

Egyptian (راح):

  • روحت (roḥt - I went)
  • روحت (roḥt - you (masc. sg.) went)
  • راح (rāḥ - he went)
  • راحوا (rāḥu - they went)
Example Sentence
  • كتبتُ رسالة. (Katabtu risālah - I wrote a letter.)
  • قرأَ الكتاب. (Qaraʾa al-kitāb - He read the book.)
  • كتبت جواب. (Katabt gawāb - I wrote a letter.)
  • قرا الكتاب. (ʾara el-kitāb - He read the book.)

8. Negation Patterns Across Different Tenses

Egyptian Arabic uses a distinctive circumfix (`ma...sh`) for verb negation, which is a key differentiator from MSA's varied negation particles.

Aspect Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) Egyptian Arabic
Verbs (Present/Past/Future)

Uses various particles depending on tense and context (لا, ما, لم, لن).

  • Example (Present): لا أذهب. (Lā adhhab - I do not go.)
  • Example (Past): ما ذهبتُ. (Mā dhahabtu - I did not go.)
  • Example (Future): لن أذهبَ. (Lan adhhab - I will not go.)

Uses the circumfix ما...ش (ma...sh) around the verb.

  • Example (Present): أنا ماباكُلش. (Ana mabakolsh - I don't eat.)
  • Example (Past): ماروحتش. (Māroḥtish - I didn't go.)
  • Example (Future): مش هاروح. (Mesh harūḥ - I won't go.) (Often 'mesh' replaces 'ma...sh' for future)
Nominal/Adjectival/Equational

Uses ليس (laysa) for nouns, adjectives, and equational sentences.

  • Example: أنا لستُ طالبًا. (Anā lastu ṭāliban - I am not a student.)

Uses مش (mish) before the word.

  • Example: أنا مش طالب. (Ana mish ṭālib - I am not a student.)

(MSA: مبروك - Mabrūk)

(MSA: الله يبارك فيك - Allāh yubārik fīk)

8. إن شاء الله (In shāʾ Allāh) - God willing, hopefully.

(MSA: إن شاء الله - In shāʾ Allāh)

9. يلا بينا (Yalla bīna) - Let's go!

(MSA: هيا بنا - Hayyā binā)

10. تمام كده؟ (Tamām kida?) - Is that right/okay?

(MSA: هل هذا صحيح؟ - Hal hādhā ṣaḥīḥ?)

11. خلاص (Khalāṣ) - Done, finished, enough.

(MSA: انتهى - Intahā)

12. بجد؟ (Bigad?) - Really? Seriously?

(MSA: حقًا؟ - Ḥaqqan?)

13. أكيد (ʾAkīd) - Certainly, surely.

(MSA: بالتأكيد - Bi-t-taʾkīd)

14. متشكر (Mitshakkir) - Thank you. (More casual than shukran, for masculine speaker.)

(MSA: شاكر - Shākir)

15. مش مشكلة (Mish mushkila) - No problem.

(MSA: لا مشكلة - Lā mushkila)

16. لا مؤاخذة (La muʾākhza) - Excuse me, pardon me (for minor things, e.g., bumping into someone).

(MSA: عفواً - ʿAfwan)

17. صباح الخير (Sabāḥ el-khēr) - Good morning.

(MSA: صباح الخير - Sabāḥ al-khayr)

18. مساء الخير (Masāʾ el-khēr) - Good evening.

(MSA: مساء الخير - Masāʾ al-khayr)

19. تصبح على خير (Tisbaḥ ʿala khēr) - Good night (to a man).

(MSA: تصبح على خير - Tuṣbiḥ ʿalā khayr)

20. نايم (Nāyim) - Asleep.

(MSA: نائم - Nāʾim)

21. صاحي (Ṣāḥi) - Awake.

(MSA: صاحٍ - Ṣāḥin)

22. بكام ده؟ (Bikām da?) - How much is this?

(MSA: بكم هذا؟ - Bikam hādhā?)

23. ربنا يخليك (Rabbena yikhallīk) - May God keep you safe/bless you. (Common expression of gratitude or affection.)

(MSA: بارك الله فيك - Bāraka Allāh fīk)

24. يا عم (Ya ʿamm) - Hey, man / dude (addressing a male informally). (Literally: "O uncle.")

(No direct MSA equivalent for this usage.)

25. يا ساتر (Ya sāter!) - Oh my God! (Expression of shock or surprise.)

(MSA: يا ساتر - Yā sātir)

26. بالهنا والشفا (Bi-l-hana wa-sh-shifa) - Bon appétit / Enjoy your meal. (Lit: "With health and healing.")

(MSA: شهية طيبة - Shahiyyah ṭayyibah)

27. مفيش مشكلة (Māfīsh mushkila) - There is no problem.

(MSA: لا توجد مشكلة - Lā tūjadu mushkila)

28. تعالى (Taʿāla) - Come here (to a male).

(MSA: تعال - Taʿāl)

29. تعالي (Taʿālī) - Come here (to a female).

(MSA: تعالي - Taʿālī)

30. استنى (Istanna) - Wait (to a male).

(MSA: انتظر - Intaẓir)

31. أنا آسف (Ana ʾāsif) - I'm sorry.

(MSA: أنا آسف - Anā ʾāsif)

32. فوق! (Fōʾ!) - Wake up! / Upstairs! (Depending on context.)

(MSA: استيقظ! - Istayqiẓ!)

33. تحت (Taḥt) - Downstairs / Under.

(MSA: تحت - Taḥt)

34. هنا (Hena) - Here.

(MSA: هنا - Hunā)

35. هناك (Henāk) - There.

(MSA: هناك - Hunāka)

36. أنا جعان (Ana gaʿān) - I'm hungry (masculine).

(MSA: أنا جائع - Anā jāʾiʿ)

37. أنا عطشان (Ana ʿaṭshān) - I'm thirsty (masculine).

(MSA: أنا عطشان - Anā ʿaṭshān)

38. عايز إيه؟ (ʿAyiz ʾēh?) - What do you want? (to a male).

(MSA: ماذا تريد؟ - Mādhā turīd?)

39. مش عايز (Mish ʿayiz) - I don't want (masculine).

(MSA: لا أريد - Lā urīd)

40. ممكن (Mumkin) - Possible / May I? / Can I?

(MSA: ممكن - Mumkin)

41. بعد إذنك (Baʿd ʾiznak) - With your permission / Excuse me (when leaving or interrupting).

(MSA: بعد إذنك - Baʿda ʾidhnika)

42. مفيش (Māfīsh) - There isn't any / Nothing.

(MSA: لا يوجد - Lā yūjadu)

43. يا ريت (Yā rēt) - I wish / If only.

(MSA: يا ليت - Yā layta)

44. تسلم إيدك (Tislam ʾīdak) - Thank you (Lit: "May your hand be safe/blessed," often said when someone serves you food or helps with manual work).

(No direct MSA equivalent)

45. زي الفل (Zayy el-full) - Excellent, perfect. (Lit: "Like jasmine.")

(MSA: ممتاز - Mumtāz)

46. ربنا معاك (Rabbena maʿāk) - God be with you.

(MSA: الله معك - Allāh maʿak)

47. بالتوفيق (Bi-t-tawfīq) - Good luck.

(MSA: بالتوفيق - Bi-t-tawfīq)

48. كده (Kida) - Like this / So.

(MSA: هكذا - Hākadhā)

49. طبعاً (Ṭabʿan) - Of course.

(MSA: طبعاً - Ṭabʿan)

50. دلوقتي (Dilwaʾti) - Now.

(MSA: الآن - Al-ʾān)
