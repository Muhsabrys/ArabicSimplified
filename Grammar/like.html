Arabic Comparison Words

مِثْل، كَـ، كَمَا، مِثْلَما، and Egyptian زَيّ ما

English often uses like or as in several different structures. In Arabic, the correct form depends mainly on what comes next: a noun, a pronoun, or a clause.

1. The basic difference

FormWhat normally follows?Main useExample
مِثْل A noun, noun phrase, demonstrative, or attached pronoun like; similar to هو مِثْلُ أَخيهِ.
He is like his brother.
كَـ A noun attached directly to it like; as; such as هو كَالأَسَدِ.
He is like a lion.
كَمَا A clause—often verbal, but not necessarily as; just as; and also اِفْعَلْ كَمَا قُلْتُ لَكَ.
Do as I told you.
مِثْلَما A clause just as; in the same way that حَدَثَ مِثْلَما تَوَقَّعْنا.
It happened just as we expected.
مِثْلَ ما ما meaning “what / that which” something like what... اِشْتَرَيْتُ مِثْلَ ما اِشْتَرَيْتَ.
I bought something like what you bought.

2. مِثْل: a noun followed by another noun or a pronoun

Main rule

مِثْل is a noun. It is commonly the first word of an إِضافَة, so the following noun is genitive. A pronoun suffix may also attach to it.

مِثْل+noun / demonstrative / pronoun suffix

Common forms: مِثْلي، مِثْلُكَ، مِثْلُكِ، مِثْلُهُ، مِثْلُها، مِثْلُنا، مِثْلُكُم، مِثْلُهُم

Important: the final vowel on مِثْل changes according to its grammatical position. In ordinary unvocalized writing, all three forms are written simply مثل.
هو مِثْلُ أَخيهِ.
nominative: predicate
أُريدُ فُسْتانًا مِثْلَ فُسْتانِكِ.
accusative: modifying the object
مَرَرْتُ بِرَجُلٍ مِثْلِ أَخيكَ.
genitive: after a preposition
أُريدُ أَنْ أَسْكُنَ في شَقَّةٍ مِثْلِ شَقَّةِ زُمَلائي.
I want to live in an apartment like my classmates’ apartment.
تَتَمَنّى أَنْ تُصْبِحَ ابْنَتُها مُهَنْدِسَةً مِثْلَها.
She hopes that her daughter will become an engineer like her.
لَيْسَ هُناكَ مَكانٌ مِثْلُ البَيْتِ.
There is no place like home.
لا أَحَدَ يَفْهَمُني مِثْلَكَ.
No one understands me like you do.
طَعْمُ هٰذا الشَّرابِ مِثْلُ اللَّيْمونِ.
This drink tastes like lemon.
في مِثْلِ هٰذِهِ الظُّروفِ، يَجِبُ أَنْ نَبْقى هادِئينَ.
In circumstances like these, we must remain calm.

3. كَـ: an attached preposition before a noun

Main rule

كَـ is attached directly to the following noun. In fully inflected Arabic, that noun is genitive.

كَـ+الأَسَد→كَالأَسَدِ
الجالِيَةُ العَرَبِيَّةُ، كَغَيْرِها مِنَ الجالِيّاتِ، تَهْتَمُّ بِالثَّقافَةِ وَاللُّغَةِ.
The Arab community, like other communities, cares about culture and language.
كانَ الطِّفْلُ يَجْري كَالرّيحِ.
The child was running like the wind.
العِلْمُ كَالنّورِ.
Knowledge is like light.
كانَ وَجْهُها كَالقَمَرِ.
Her face was like the moon.
تَحْتاجُ مُدُنٌ كَبيرَةٌ، كَالقاهِرَةِ وَشيكاغو، إِلى نِظامِ نَقْلٍ فَعّالٍ.
Large cities, such as Cairo and Chicago, need an effective transportation system.
هذا القُماشُ ناعِمٌ كَالحَريرِ.
This fabric is soft like silk.
For learners: do not normally use كَـ with an independent pronoun such as أنا or أنت. Standard prose normally uses مِثْلي and مِثْلُكَ. Rare poetic or disputed constructions are not useful for everyday production.

4. كَمَا: before a clause

Main rule

كَمَا introduces a clause and commonly means as or just as. The clause is often verbal, but Arabic also uses patterns such as كما هو... and كما أنَّ....

كَمَا+clause
المَوْضوعُ لَيْسَ بَسيطًا كَمَا تَقولينَ.
The issue is not as simple as you say.
كَمَا تَعْلَمونَ، الاِمْتِحانُ سَيَكونُ غَدًا.
As you know, the exam will be tomorrow.
اِكْتُبِ الجُمْلَةَ كَمَا كَتَبْتُها عَلى السَّبّورَةِ.
Write the sentence as I wrote it on the board.
بَقِيَ الوَضْعُ كَمَا هُوَ.
The situation remained as it was.
القِراءَةُ مُفيدَةٌ، كَمَا أَنَّها مُمتِعَةٌ أَيْضًا.
Reading is useful, and it is also enjoyable.
تُوَسِّعُ القِراءَةُ المُفْرَداتِ، كَمَا تُساعِدُ عَلى تَحْسينِ الكِتابَةِ.
Reading expands vocabulary and also helps improve writing.
Here كما has an additive meaning: “and also.”

5. مِثْلَما versus مِثْلَ ما

مِثْلَما — written together

Use it as a connector meaning just as or in the same way that.

حَدَثَ كُلُّ شَيْءٍ مِثْلَما تَوَقَّعْنا.
Everything happened just as we expected.
عامِلِ النّاسَ مِثْلَما تُحِبُّ أَنْ يُعامِلوكَ.
Treat people as you would like them to treat you.

مِثْلَ ما — written separately

Write the words separately when ما means what or that which. In that structure, مِثْل keeps its normal grammatical role in the sentence.

اِشْتَرَيْتُ مِثْلَ ما اِشْتَرَيْتَ.
I bought something like what you bought.
قالوا مِثْلَ ما قالَ الأَوَّلونَ.
They said the same kind of thing that earlier people had said.

6. Egyptian Arabic: زَيّ and زَيّ ما

زَيّ + noun/pronoun: الفُسْتان دَه زَيّ فُسْتانِك. / هُوَّ زَيَّك.

زَيّ ما + clause: اِعْمِل زَيّ ما قُلْت لَك.

Another example: المَوْضوع مِش بَسيط زَيّ ما بْتِقولي.

Dialect spelling is not completely standardized. This page uses زَيّ and زَيّ ما consistently.

7. Quick decision guide

A noun or pronoun follows: use مِثْل.

A formal attached comparison before a noun: use كَـ.

A clause follows: use كَمَا or مِثْلَما.

ما means “what / that which”: write مِثْلَ ما as two words.

Everyday Egyptian: use زَيّ before nouns/pronouns and زَيّ ما before clauses.

9. Speaking practice

Compare two people

Describe two family members or classmates. Use مِثْل twice, including one attached pronoun.

Give instructions

Explain a process. Use كَمَا in at least three instructions.

Create vivid comparisons

Describe a person, city, or weather scene using three comparisons with كَـ.

Role-play: choosing clothes

One student is a customer and the other is a friend or salesperson. Compare colors, patterns, prices, and styles using مِثْل, كَـ, and كَمَا.
