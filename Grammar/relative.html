Relative Pronouns in Formal and Spoken Arabic

Learn how Arabic links a definite noun to a sentence-length description, how formal relative pronouns agree with gender and number, and how spoken Arabic commonly uses اللّي.

صَديقَتي لَيْلى هِيَ الشَّخْصُ الوَحيدُ الَّذي أَتَكَلَّمُ مَعَهُ عَنْ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ.
My friend Laila is the only person whom I talk with about everything.
Warm-up and noticing

What did you hear?

The same idea can be expressed in formal Arabic and in spoken varieties. Notice the linking word in each sentence.

Formal Arabic
صَديقَتي لَيْلى هِيَ الشَّخْصُ الوَحيدُ الَّذي أَتَكَلَّمُ مَعَهُ عَنْ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ.
Egyptian Arabic
صاحْبِتي لَيْلى هِيَّ الشَّخْص الوَحيد اللّي بَتْكَلِّم مَعاه عَنْ كُلّ حاجَة.
Levantine Arabic
رَفيقْتي نور هِيّ الشَّخْص الوَحيد اللّي بَحْكي مَعُه عَنْ كُلّ شِي.
Formal Arabic changes the relative pronoun according to the noun. Many spoken dialects use one invariant form, اللّي, for masculine, feminine, singular, and plural nouns.
Connecting to familiar grammar

Review: جُمْلَةُ الصِّفَة

A sentence can describe an indefinite noun directly. In this structure, Arabic does not use a relative pronoun.

مُها لَها خالَةٌ تَسْكُنُ في كاليفورنيا.
Maha has an aunt who lives in California.
تَعَرَّفْتُ عَلى طالِبَةٍ تَجْلِسُ بِجانِبي في الصَّفِّ.
I met a student who sits beside me in class.
هُمْ زُمَلاءُ أَعْرِفُهُمْ مِنَ العَمَلِ.
They are colleagues whom I know from work.
اِسْمٌ نَكِرَةٌ + جُمْلَةٌ تَصِفُهُ
The descriptive sentence functions like an adjective because the noun is indefinite: خالَةٌ، طالِبَةٍ، زُمَلاءُ.
From indefinite to definite

A Definite Noun Needs a Linking Word

When the noun is definite and the description is a full sentence, formal Arabic introduces the description with a relative pronoun.

Indefinite noun: no relative pronoun Definite noun: use a relative pronoun
لَها خالَةٌ تَسْكُنُ في كاليفورنيا. هٰذِهِ هِيَ الخالَةُ الَّتي تَسْكُنُ في كاليفورنيا.
تَعَرَّفْتُ عَلى طالِبَةٍ تَجْلِسُ بِجانِبي. هٰذِهِ هِيَ الطّالِبَةُ الَّتي تَجْلِسُ بِجانِبي.
هُمْ زُمَلاءُ أَعْرِفُهُمْ مِنَ العَمَلِ. هٰؤُلاءِ هُمُ الزُّمَلاءُ الَّذينَ أَعْرِفُهُمْ مِنَ العَمَلِ.
اِسْمٌ مَعْرِفَةٌ + اِسْمٌ مَوْصولٌ + صِلَةُ المَوْصولِ

High-frequency formal forms

The Three Forms to Master First

These forms cover singular masculine, singular feminine, and masculine or mixed human plural nouns.

singular masculine
الَّذي
الرَّجُلُ الَّذي يَبْحَثُ عَنْ حِذاءٍ
singular feminine
الَّتي
المَرْأَةُ الَّتي تَلْبَسُ تَنّورَةً
masculine or mixed human plural
الَّذينَ
المُهاجِرونَ الَّذينَ يَبْحَثونَ عَنْ عَمَلٍ
The relative pronoun agrees with the noun before it, much like an adjective agrees with its noun.

Dual reminder

الشَّخْصانِ اللَّذانِ يَحْمِلانِ الحَقائِبَ

Do not decide from English

English uses “who/which/that” without gender agreement. Arabic chooses the form from the Arabic noun.

Important agreement rule

Non-Human Plurals Use الَّتي

Broken and sound plurals referring to non-human entities are normally treated as feminine singular for agreement.

الكُتُبُ الَّتي اِشْتَرَيْتُها مُفيدَةٌ.
The books that I bought are useful.
المَلابِسُ الَّتي لَبِسْتُها لَمْ تَكُنْ مُناسِبَةً.
The clothes that I wore were not suitable.
القِطَطُ الَّتي تَلْعَبُ جَميلَةٌ.
The cats that are playing are beautiful.
Use الَّتي, not الَّذينَ or اللّاتي, because the plural is non-human.
Inside the relative clause

صِلَةُ المَوْصولِ and عائِدُ المَوْصولِ

The relative clause often contains a pronoun that refers back to the noun. This is especially visible when the noun is the object of a verb or follows a preposition inside the clause.

Relative noun as subject

المُهاجِرونَ الجُدُدُ الَّذينَ جاؤوا إلى بَلَدِنا يَبْحَثونَ عَنْ عَمَلٍ.
The relative noun is the subject of “came”; the plural ending carries the subject reference.

Relative noun as object

الشَّخْصُ الوَحيدُ الَّذي أَعْرِفُهُ هُنا هُوَ أَنْتَ.
The suffix ـه is the object pronoun referring back to الشخص.

After a preposition

المُهاجِرونَ الَّذينَ تَكَلَّمْتُ مَعَهُمْ لَيْسوا سُعَداءَ هُنا.

Clothing example

المَلابِسُ البَسيطَةُ الَّتي لَبِسْتُها لَمْ تَكُنْ مُناسِبَةً.

٢. المَلابِسُ الَّتي لَبِسْتُها لَمْ تَكُنْ مُناسِبَةً.
٣. المُهاجِرونَ الَّذينَ تَكَلَّمْتُ مَعَهُمْ لَيْسوا سُعَداءَ.
٤. الحِذاءُ الَّذي بَحَثْتُ عَنْهُ غالٍ جِدًّا.
Ask: “Where is the noun represented inside the relative clause?” The answer may be a verb suffix, a pronoun after a preposition, or an understood subject.

Register comparison

Formal Arabic versus Spoken Arabic

Formal Arabic distinguishes gender and number. Many spoken varieties use اللّي for all antecedents.

Antecedent Formal Arabic Common spoken form
singular masculine الشَّخْصُ الَّذي الشَّخْص اللّي
singular feminine المَرْأَةُ الَّتي المَرَة / السِّتّ اللّي
human plural المُهاجِرونَ الَّذينَ المُهاجْرين اللّي
non-human plural المَلابِسُ الَّتي الهُدوم / التِّياب اللّي
Levantine Arabic may also use يَلّي. The key point is that the spoken linker generally does not change for gender or number.

Nonspecific or free relatives

ما: “What” or “Whatever”

ما can refer to an unspecified thing or idea. It does not agree for gender or number.

ما
لَيْلى تَفْهَمُ ما أَشْعُرُ بِهِ.
Laila understands what I feel.
ما
والِدَتي تَسْمَحُ لي بِأَنْ أَلْبَسَ ما أُريدُ.
My mother allows me to wear whatever I want.
In ما أَشْعُرُ بِهِ, the verb شَعَرَ بِـ requires a preposition. The pronoun in بِهِ refers back to ما.
People and a spelling distinction

مَنْ: “Who” or “Whoever”

مَنْ
أَظُنُّ أَنَّكُمْ تَعْرِفونَ مَنْ أَقْصِدُ.
I think you know whom I mean.
لَيْلى تَخْرُجُ مَعَ مَنْ تُريدُ.
Laila goes out with whomever she wants.

Do not confuse these

Form Meaning and pattern
مَنْ relative word meaning who/whomever; usually introduces a clause
مِنْ preposition meaning from; followed by a noun or pronoun
أَعْرِفُ مَنْ جاءَ
رَجَعْتُ مِنْ الجامِعَةِ

Lesson summary

  • An indefinite noun can be followed directly by جملة صفة.
  • A definite noun uses an agreeing formal relative pronoun.
  • The relative clause may contain عائد الموصول.
  • Many dialects use invariant اللّي.
  • ما means what/whatever, and مَنْ means who/whoever.
