Arabic Case Endings & Verb Moods

A visual, color-coded dashboard mapping Marfūʿ (Nominative/Indicative), Manṣūb (Accusative/Subjunctive), Majrūr (Genitive), and Majzūm (Jussive) grammatical structures.

The Big Idea

In Arabic, the ending of a word tells us its grammatical job. For nouns, the ending shows case: nominative, accusative, or genitive. For present-tense verbs, the ending shows mood: indicative, subjunctive, or jussive.

The default form is usually Marfūʿ. A noun is usually nominative when it is the subject or predicate. A present-tense verb is usually nominative/indicative when no special particle comes before it.

Quick Decision Guide

  1. Is the word a noun after a preposition?
    Use Majrūr.
    في البيتِ
  2. Is the word the second part of an iḍāfa?
    Use Majrūr.
    بيتُ الطالبِ
  3. Is the noun an object, adverbial expression, tamyīz, or predicate of Kānah?
    Use Manṣūb.
    قرأتُ الكتابَ
  4. Is the noun a subject or predicate in a basic sentence?
    Use Marfūʿ.
    الطالبُ مجتهدٌ
  5. Is the present-tense verb after An, Lan, Kay, Ḥattā, or Li- (lam al-ta'leel)?
    Use Manṣūb.
    أريدُ أن أدرسَ
  6. Is the present-tense verb after Lam, Lā of prohibition, Li of command, or a conditional particle?
    Use Majzūm.
    لم أذهبْ
  7. If none of these applies to a present-tense verb, use the default.
    Use Marfūʿ.
    أنا أدرسُ

Marfūʿ Nominative / Indicative

Marfūʿ is the default for many nouns and present-tense verbs. For nouns, it often marks the subject or predicate. For present-tense verbs, it is used when no particle changes the verb.

For Nouns

Common uses:

  • Subject of a nominal sentence (Al-Mubtada')
  • Predicate of a nominal sentence (Al-Khabar)
  • Subject after a verb (Al-Fā'il)

For Verbs

Common use:

  • Present-tense verb with no particle before it.
  • The “five verbs” keep the noon (ن): يدرسون، تدرسين، تذهبان
Type Arabic Example Explanation
Noun: subject الطلابُ يدرسون. The word Al-Ṭullābu is the subject, so it is nominative.
Noun: subject after verb يدرسُ الطلابُ. The sentence begins with a verb, but Al-Ṭullābu is still the subject.
Noun: subject and predicate الطقسُ باردٌ. Both the subject and the predicate are nominative in a basic nominal sentence.
Noun: sound masculine plural المصريونَ يحبون كرةَ القدم. The word Al-Miṣriyyūna is nominative because it is the subject. Sound masculine plural nouns use the oon ending in the nominative case.
Verb: no particle before it أنا أسافرُ في النهار. The verb Usāfiru is a present-tense verb with no particle before it.
Verb: no particle before it أخي يخرجُ في الليل. The verb Yakhruju is indicative because nothing comes before it to change it.
Verb: five verbs أصدقائي يخرجون معًا كلَّ يوم. The verb Yakhrujūna keeps the noon (ن) because it is not preceded by a changing particle.
Verb: plural subject أنا وأختي نكتبُ الواجبَ. The verb Naktubu is present indicative.

Manṣūb Accusative / Subjunctive

Manṣūb appears with both nouns and present-tense verbs. With verbs, it is used after certain particles. With nouns, it is used for objects, many adverbial expressions, tamyīz, and the predicate of Kānah.

Important: For verbs, particles like An, Lan, Kay, Ḥattā, and Li- make the present-tense verb Manṣūb.
Use Arabic Example Explanation
Verb after An أختي تحبُّ أنْ تدرسَ في المساءِ. The particle An makes the verb Tadrusa subjunctive.
Verb after Lan نحنُ لن نسافرَ اليومَ. The particle Lan makes the present verb Manṣūb.
Verb after Kay سافرتُ كي أدرسَ في الخارجِ. The particle Kay means “in order to” and makes the verb Manṣūb.
Verb after Ḥattā اتصلتُ بأختي حتى أقولَ لها عيدًا سعيدًا. The particle Ḥattā can mean “in order to” here and makes the verb Manṣūb.
Verb after Li- جئتُ إلى أمريكا لأتعلمَ. The attached Li- here means “in order to,” so the verb becomes Manṣūb.
Object كتبَ الطالبُ الدرسَ. The word Al-Darsa is the direct object of the verb.
Object قرأتُ الكتابَ في المكتبةِ. The word Al-Kitāba is what I read, so it takes the accusative case.
Adverb of time نمتُ ساعةً ثم سافرتُ. The word Sā'atan tells how long I slept adverbially.
Adverb of place/direction سافرتُ جنوبًا. The word Janūban specifies direction/location adverbially.
Tamyīz: distinguishing word دفعتُ عشرينَ دولارًا. The word Dūlāran clarifies what structural number "twenty" refers to.
Tamyīz: measurement سقتُ السيارةَ عشرينَ كيلومترًا. The word Kīlūmitran clarifies the unit of distance measurement.
Predicate of Kānah كان الطقسُ باردًا. After Kānah, the subject stays nominative, but the predicate becomes accusative.
Predicate of Kānah كان أخي مريضًا. The word Marīḍan is the predicate of Kānah.
Predicate of Kānah (Feminine) كانت صديقتي مسافرةً. The word Musāfiratan is the predicate of Kānat.
Predicate of Kānah كان الطالبُ غائبًا. The subject stays nominative, while the word Ghā'iban shifts to accusative.
Correction note: We say دفعتُ عشرينَ دولارًا, not دفعتُ عشرينًا دولارًا. The specifier word دولارًا is what carries the accusative tanween.

Majrūr Genitive

Majrūr applies strictly to nouns. Verbs can never be genitive. The two most common structural triggers are prepositions and iḍāfa compound phrases.

Important: There are absolutely no Majrūr verbs. If you are analyzing a verb structure, skip this entire category.

Common Prepositions

في، من، عن، على، إلى، لـ، بـ، بين، فوق، تحت، أمام، خلف، مع

Use Arabic Example Explanation
After Fee (في) أنا في البيتِ. The word Al-Bayti is genitive because it directly follows a preposition.
After Min (من) نحنُ من القاهرةِ. The word Al-Qāhirati is genitive because it follows a preposition.
After Bayna (بين) سأقابلكَ بين الخامسةِ والسادسةِ. Both time descriptors are genitive because they link back structurally to the word "between".
After multiple prepositions حصلَ الطالبُ على هديةٍ من المدرسين. The word Hadiyyatin follows one preposition, and Al-Mudarrisīna follows another.
After Li- (لـ) اشتريتُ كتابًا للطالبِ. The prefix Li- attached directly to a noun operates as a preposition.
Iḍāfa: second word هذا كتابُ الطالبِ. The second noun in a standard possessive compound link is always genitive.
Iḍāfa after a preposition أعيشُ في بيتِ الطلابِ. The word Bayti is genitive after a preposition, while Al-Ṭullābi is genitive because it functions as the second word in the phrase.
Iḍāfa inside an object عرفتُ اسمَ المدينةِ. The word Isma is accusative because it is the main object, but Al-Madīnati is genitive as the second component of the phrase.

Majzūm Jussive

Majzūm applies strictly to present-tense verbs. Nouns can never be jussive.

The most common jussive tool is the particle Lam. It functions as past-tense negation ("did not") and changes the following present verb to the jussive form.
Use Arabic Example Explanation
After Lam لم يذهبْ. The particle Lam puts the verb into the jussive, giving it a sukūn ending.
After Lam لم أسافرْ أمسِ. The verb Usāfir takes a sukūn because of the preceding negative particle.
Five verbs: remove noon (ن) هم لم يسافروا. The original default verb was Yusāfirūna. After Lam, the ending noon is deleted.
Five verbs: remove noon (ن) أنتِ لم تدرsi. The original default verb was Tadrusīna. After Lam, the ending noon is deleted.
Command prefix (Li-) لْنكتبْ الواجبَ الآن. The command prefix forces the present-tense verb directly into the jussive.
Negative commands لا تخرجْ متأخرًا. The prohibitive particle Lā puts the action into a jussive command state.

Practice

Read each sentence. Decide whether the highlighted word should be Marfūʿ, Manṣūb, Majrūr, or Majzūm.

  1. الطالبُ يكتبُ الدرسَ في البيتِ.
  2. أريدُ أن أدرسَ العربيةَ في الجامعةِ.
  3. لم يسافرْ أخي أمسِ.
  4. كان الجوُّ جميلًا.
  5. اشتريتُ كتابَ الطالبِ.
  6. نحنُ لن نخرجَ الليلةَ.
  7. المعلمون يشرحون الدرسَ.
  8. سافرتُ عشرينَ ميلًا.
View Answer Key
  1. Al-Ṭullābu: Marfūʿ because it is the sentence subject (Mubtada').
    Yaktubu: Marfūʿ because it is a present verb with no altering particles.
    Al-Darsa: Manṣūb because it is the direct object (Maf'ūl bihi).
    Al-Bayti: Majrūr because it follows the preposition Fee.
  2. Adrasa: Manṣūb because it follows the particle An.
    Al-ʿArabiyyata: Manṣūb because it is the direct object.
    Al-Jāmiʿati: Majrūr because it follows the preposition Fee.
  3. Yusāfir: Majzūm because it follows the negative particle Lam.
    Akhī: Marfūʿ because it is the verb's subject (Fā'il).
  4. Al-Jawwu: Marfūʿ because it is the subject name of Kānah (Ism Kānah).
    Jamīlan: Manṣūb because it is the predicate statement of Kānah (Khabar Kānah).
  5. Kitāba: Manṣūb because it is the direct object.
    Al-Ṭālibi: Majrūr because it is the possessive second half of the iḍāfa compound (Mudāf ilayh).
  6. Nakhruja: Manṣūb because it follows the future negation particle Lan.
    Al-Laylata: Manṣūb because it functions as an adverbial time placement marker.
  7. Al-Muʿallimūna: Marfūʿ because it is the plural sentence subject.
    Yashraḥūna: Marfūʿ because it is a present verb with no prefix particle, meaning it preserves its noon character.
    Al-Darsa: Manṣūb because it acts as the direct object.
  8. Mīlan: Manṣūb because it functions as a unit specifier expression (Tamyīz).

Common Student Mistakes

Mistake Better Form Why?
أنا في البيتَ. أنا في البيتِ. Following standard prepositions, the noun shifts into the genitive state (Majrūr).
أريدُ أن أدرسُ. أريدُ أن أدرسَ. Directly following the particle An, a present-tense verb becomes subjunctive (Manṣūb).
لم يذهبُ. لم يذهبْ. Directly following the particle Lam, a present-tense verb converts to jussive (Majzūm).
كان الطالبُ غائبٌ. كان الطالبُ غائبًا. The active predicate statement of any Kānah construction is always accusative (Manṣūb).
بيتُ الطالبُ كبيرٌ. بيتُ الطالبِ كبيرٌ. The second noun in any standard possessive iḍāfa structural pairing is genitive (Majrūr).
